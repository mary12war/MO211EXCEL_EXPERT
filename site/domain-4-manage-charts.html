Manage Charts

Use advanced chart types and formatting choices to turn data into accurate visual stories.

35 minutesIntermediateTopics: combo charts, trendlines, dual axes

Real-World Hook

Your summary numbers are correct, but leadership will only understand the story if you pick a chart type that makes comparisons and trends instantly visible.

Common Misconception

⚠️ Students often think "prettier" charts are better charts; in exam settings, the best chart is the one that answers the question with the fewest interpretation errors.

Mastery Steps

  1. Compare categories with columns, show trends over time with lines, and combine series only when two scales are truly needed.

    Task: Decide whether a dashboard prompt needs a line, column, or combo chart.

  2. Add secondary axes, trendlines, and data labels only when they improve interpretation.

    Task: Build a dual-axis chart for revenue and units sold.

  3. Simplify legends, titles, and colors so the chart can be read in a glance.

    Task: Remove chart junk and rewrite the chart title as a takeaway sentence.

  4. Ask what conclusion a viewer would draw in five seconds, then adjust the chart if the answer is fuzzy.

    Task: Explain the trendline story out loud in one sentence.

Worked Example

Plot revenue as columns and units sold as a line on a secondary axis, then add a trendline to reveal whether growth is steady or seasonal.

Use the sample budget data file to compare budget versus actual values by department before turning that comparison into a combo chart lesson.

Teacher Tip

💡 Before students click Insert Chart, make them answer this sentence frame: "I want my audience to notice ______." The chart choice usually becomes obvious.
